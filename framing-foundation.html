01 — THE PROBLEM

Said plainly, then said precisely

In one sentence

The most powerful way to work with a coding agent — having it make things you look at and react to — silently breaks the moment that work leaves your machine: you can read the source, but you can't see the rendered thing.

The setup: a real way of working

The way to get the most out of a coding agent is to have it make things you can look at and react to, not walls of text. A rendered plan. An HTML prototype. A formatted brief. A mockup. A diagram. Agents are good at this now — "HTML is all you need" is a genuine, productive practice, not a gimmick. You think faster when you're reacting to a thing than to a paragraph describing it.

Where it works: exactly one situation

This loop is magic when it's you, alone, at your own machine — where localhost and your editor's preview are right there. Open the file, look, react, iterate. No friction.

Where it breaks: the moment the work has to travel

  • Your agent is running in the cloud — a remote dev box, a background agent — and there's no localhost you can reach.
  • Your review is on your phone — you tap the link the agent gave you and get a dead localhost:5173, or a wall of raw HTML.
  • The next step belongs to someone else — a teammate, or the next agent — and all you can hand them is a diff or a screenshot.

The pain underneath all of it

You can see the code of what your agent made.
You just can't see the thing.

A diff
Source you can read, not view. You're staring at HTML tags and Markdown syntax — never the rendered page.
Markdown, remotely
Raw text. No formatted viewer on the web or a phone — it arrives unrendered.
An HTML plan
No way to open it. The whole "HTML is all you need" technique falls apart when you can't load the file.
On a cloud box
Out of reach, then gone. Anything not in the diff is stranded on the machine that made it — and lost when the session ends.

So the richest part of working with an agent — the look-at-it-and-react loop — quietly stops working the instant you're not sitting at the machine that made it.

The one-line wedge we keep coming back to:
"Local preview works great — until the work isn't local."

02 — THE DISTINCTION WE KEEP LOSING

This is not your product. It's the work around the code.

The single thing the framing keeps dropping: the stuff we're talking about is not your software. It's the working material the agent makes on the way to the code — plans, prototypes, briefs, mockups, specs, diagrams, explorations. It was never meant to be committed, deployed, or handed off as a deliverable.

Think of the interim boards and clickable prototypes you keep in Figma and never hand to a developer — they're how you get to the design, and it's fine for them to be disposable. Agents make exactly this kind of throwaway exploration, constantly, and have no canvas for it.

Your repo holds the product. This is everything the agent makes to build it. That's why none of the existing homes fit — they're all built for source or for shipping.

Why this matters for copy: "preview" begs the question preview of what? — and the honest, differentiating answer is "the stuff that isn't your code." If we don't say that, we get mistaken for a deploy tool or a pastebin.

03 — WHO HAS IT, AND WHEN IT BITES

The audience is defined by a moment, not a job title

It bites the instant agent work has to leave the machine that made it:

  • Cloud / background agents — the agent runs somewhere you're not.
  • Mobile / away-from-desk review — checking the work from your phone.
  • Remote dev boxes & web-based agents — SSH, containers, Claude Code on the web.
  • Async collaboration — handing agent output to a teammate.
  • Multi-agent workflows — one agent's artifact is the next agent's input.
  • "HTML is all you need" practitioners — they generate the most look-at-able artifacts, so they feel this most acutely.

Honest scoping: if you work purely solo, locally, and never share, local preview is genuinely fine. We're not for that moment — and saying so keeps us from overclaiming.

04 — WHY IT MATTERS

When the work leaves your machine, the whole way of working breaks.

The make-look-react loop doesn't just degrade — it stops. And it stops exactly where agents are heading: the cloud, your phone, the next agent. Each way it breaks is its own cost:

  • The make-look-react loop just stops. The single best way of working with an agent — make something, look at it, react — vanishes the instant you're remote or mobile, exactly where it'd be most valuable. So you give up and fall back to walls of text.
  • A diff or screenshot isn't the thing — so you review worse. Source isn't the rendered page; a screenshot is static and you can't compare versions. You judge the work through a lossy proxy, or don't look at all — and decide worse either way.
  • You can't hand it off — not slowly, at all. Pass the plan to another thread, another agent, a teammate, or just reference it later? It doesn't exist anywhere they can reach, and agents can't see each other's artifacts. That blocks exactly the async and multi-agent workflows growing fastest.
  • Then it's gone. Ephemeral sessions and sandboxes get reclaimed; whatever didn't make it into the repo dies with the session. (The smaller one — but real.)

05 — WHAT PEOPLE DO TODAY

The alternatives, and why each falls short

Today's moveWhy it falls short
Commit it to the repoIt's throwaway — pollutes git history with things never meant to be source.
localhost / local previewDies the instant you leave the machine; invisible from a phone or a cloud box.
Screenshot / paste in chatStatic and lossy — you can't click or interact, and it isn't the real artifact.
Read the PR diffSource, not rendered. You can read it; you can't view the thing it describes.
Deploy to Vercel/NetlifyHeavyweight and built for shipping products — config, permanence, ends up in the repo. Wrong tool for scratch.
A vendor's artifact panelLocked to one tool; doesn't travel across agents or become a stable, shareable URL.

The gap: there's no lightweight, shareable, throwaway home for the work between the prompt and the product.

06 — WHAT IT ACTUALLY IS

The thing itself, plainly

protocontent gives every artifact a coding agent makes a live, shareable URL the instant it's created — so the working material is viewable, shareable, and ready to hand off from anywhere, on any device, across any agent. Each session also gets one live page that updates as the agent works. Artifacts are sandboxed (isolated from your stuff), ephemeral by default (they expire; keep the ones you want), and never in your repo.

Said as a role: it's the place the agent's working material shows up so you can see it, react to it, and pass it on — the canvas the look-at-and-react loop needs once you're not at the machine.

07 — NUANCES TO PRESERVE WHEN WE WRITE THE LINE

The things the copy must not drop

  • Working material, not the product. The repo holds the product; this holds the stuff on the way to it.
  • Answer "preview of what?" — name the non-code artifacts, in the line itself.
  • It's a way of working ("look at what it makes, don't read what it types") that protocontent unlocks beyond the local desk.
  • Signal the "HTML-plans" practice — speak to people who already work this way.
  • Name the wedge: the value appears when the work isn't local.
  • Don't overclaim. The honest boundary is "when work leaves your machine."
  • Pain + category together. "Scratchpad" is a great noun for what it is but undersells the problem; the chosen line has to make the pain land too.

08 — WHAT IT IS NOT

Non-goals (so the framing stays honest)

✗Not a deploy/hosting platform for finished products.
✗Not git, a CMS, or an archive — ephemeral by default.
✗Not a single-vendor panel — it works across agents.
✗Not "publish HTML" — that's a mechanism, not the point.
