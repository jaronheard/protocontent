Three ways to market it

Same idea — work with your agent by looking at what it makes, not reading what it types — pitched in three voices. The friction is always the same: solo on your desktop it just works; the moment the work travels to your phone, a remote run, or another agent, you're stuck with raw Markdown, unopenable HTML, and diffs you can't view.

Sam Altmancalm · visionary · understated

The best way to work with an agent is to look at what it makes.

Something has shifted in how we work with these models. The best sessions aren't walls of text — they're things you can see: a prototype, a plan, a brief, rendered and ready to react to. On your own machine, this already feels like the future. protocontent makes it work everywhere else — your phone, a remote run, the next agent in the chain. A shared place for everything your agents make to think.

"This is what working with agents should feel like."

Nikita Bierviral · punchy · consumer hook

Your agent did all that work. You literally can't see it.

Your agent just built a prototype, wrote a whole plan, designed a page. What did it hand you? A wall of HTML you can't open and a diff you can't tap. On your phone? lol. protocontent gives everything your agent makes a real link — tap it, look at it, react, keep moving. Your agent's doing design work now. Act like it.

"Stop reading your agent's homework. Just look at it."

Guillermo Rauchdeveloper-aesthetic · primitives · DX

Preview deployments, for everything your agent makes.

Coding agents are great at making things to look at — HTML prototypes, rendered plans, formatted briefs. But a local file or a diff isn't something you can open, especially from your phone or a remote session. protocontent gives every artifact a live, shareable URL the moment it's made — like preview deployments, but for the working material on the way to the code. Open it anywhere. Share it with anyone. Hand it to the next agent.

"Every artifact your agent makes, at a URL."

Quick read: Rauch's "preview deployments for your agent's working material" best carries both the look-at-it payoff and the working-material-not-product distinction — an analogy devs already trust. Altman best captures the "this is a better way to work" thesis. Bier is the strongest hook, but leans on pain over category.
